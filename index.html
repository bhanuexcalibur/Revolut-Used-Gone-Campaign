Part 01
Define the strategy
Audience Insight
They don’t fear fraud. They fear the threat they can’t see coming.
Revolut’s existing customers, digitally-native 25–38 year olds, are not naive. They’ve read the breach headlines. What they carry is a specific, low-grade dread: “the card number I gave that website two years ago is sitting in some database somewhere. I have no idea what’s going to happen with it.”

That’s not fear of a visible threat. It’s anxiety about lost control, the paranoia of invisible exposure. Competitors have been selling reassurance for 20 years. Revolut’s audience doesn’t want reassurance. They want proof there is nothing left to steal.
Positioning Statement
The only card that destroys itself before anyone else can.
Every other card protects you after something goes wrong. Revolut’s Virtual Single-Use Card makes the threat irrelevant before it begins.

Framed as security, we are one of many. Framed as control, we stand alone. Security implies a threat that needs managing. Control implies a decision you already made. No credit card, challenger bank, or traditional issuer can hold this ground. It is available. We should take it.
Core Value Proposition
Every card number you’ve ever shared is still out there. This one won’t be.
01
One use. Then it’s gone.
After a single transaction the number ceases to exist. Merchants can’t re-charge. Hackers can’t exploit it. Databases have nothing to store. Nothing left to steal, because it is simply no longer there.
02
Already on every plan. Free.
No new account, no application, no credit check. Any Revolut user creates a card in seconds at no cost. The barrier to the first use is near zero, which means the brief is a marketing problem, not a product problem.
03
Makes permanent card numbers feel reckless.
Amex, Visa, Mastercard all require a number that lives forever. Once you use a card that disappears, handing a permanent number to a merchant starts to feel like an oversight.

The thinking
How the decisions got made
→
Where the idea came from
The audience is anxious about exposure they cannot see or control. So the concept removes the thing worth stealing. Not a promise of protection: a product that makes protection irrelevant. When there is no number left, there is no attack surface. Only Revolut can make that argument.
→
Why it looks the way it looks
Every competitor in this space reaches for shields and padlocks. The brief called this out directly. So the visual language does the opposite: built on absence. Dark fields. Redacted numbers. Things that are not there. The card’s disappearance is the product and the proof. Nothing in fintech has used absence as the hero visual before.
→
Why the tagline is three words
Spent. Gone. Safe. It’s a sequence: the action, the result, the outcome. The rhythm mirrors the speed of the transaction. It works on a billboard, in a push notification, in a social caption, and as a spoken line. Shortening it loses the sequence. Lengthening it loses the punch. Three was the only answer.
Part 03 · A
Visual moodboard — campaign look and feel
Controlled Absence
The visual language is built on what is not there. Dark fields. Card numbers that dissolve, redact, and cease to exist. Revolut Blue as the only warmth in a void. Acid Green (#C8FF00) appears only at the moment of confirmation: the card is used and the number is already gone. The emptiness is the proof.

Part 03 · B
Two mock proposals
Mock 01 · In-Product Moment
The card confirms it’s gone before the receipt loads
When a single-use card transaction clears, the app shows the card number redacting itself, one group at a time, with a one-line confirmation. The product already destroys the card invisibly. Making the destruction visible turns a routine moment into a satisfying proof of control and creates a habit loop that drives repeat use.

The Redacted Receipt: breach-reactive OOH
